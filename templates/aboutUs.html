About ALS
Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) is a progressive neurodegenerative disease that affects as many as 30,000 patients in the United States every year. As the disease progresses, atrophy and muscle weakness spread to various parts of the body. Patients may develop issues with moving, swallowing, speaking, or forming words. Due to rapid progression in the severity of the disease, early diagnosis of ALS is critical to ease symptoms in the patients and provide specialized care. While there is no cure for ALS, early detection of the disease allows individuals to accurately manage symptoms, through methods such as physical, occupational, respiratory, and nutritional therapies. However, due to the symptomatological similarities between ALS and other neurodegenerative diseases, it is often difficult for patients to obtain an accurate diagnosis at an early stage of the disease. Split-hand index—a measurement of the ratio between the loss of motor ability in the pincer grasp and other, spared fingers—is a new and reliable metric for distinguishing ALS from other “mimic” disorders. But current models for detection of split-hand index involve muscle conduction and professional medical equipment unavailable in many medical facilities. A new, wearable, and user-friendly device that detects split-hand index without the utilization of electrical stimulation may aid in early detection of ALS for patient, as well as counteract early muscle compromisation in the hands.
Our Mission
Design, build, and test a new and improved diagnostic method for measuring split-hand index will be designed for early detection of ALS without the constraints of nerve conduction or electrical stimulation. The device will track the ratio of tremors between the abductor pollicis brevis and first dorsal interosseous muscles, and the abductor digiti minimi in order to identify early motor neuron degeneration from ALS while distinguishing ALS from tremor symptoms of other diseases. The device will also record and counteract the wavelengths of tremors in support of the patient’s hand. An accelerometer will be used to measure vibrations and send electrical signals. A vibration motor will be utilized to provide vibrations equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to counteract the tremors. The device will take the form of sleek yet thick fitness gloves with Arduino and strain gauges attached.
